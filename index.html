Who were the witches of Ayshire?

Jonet Slowane

Jonet Slowane was tried for witchcraft in 1658. She had originally been accused in 1651. She was accused of malificium, magical acts intended to cause harm or death to people or property. She was also said to be demonic as she had a temper, cursed and argued. Jonet was one of the last to be tried in the town of Ayr. 14 men and 10 women testified against Jonet, tragically one of the accusers was her daughter. She was tried at the Tollbooth in 1659. During her year imprisoned she was discovered to have the devil's mark. This would have involved her being pierced repeatedly with a 3 inch pin to identify an area that didn't discolour when poked. During witch trials, torture was entirely legal and she would have been deprived of sleep for days causing her to become delirious enough when provoked to implicate others in witchcraft. She was convicted and sentenced to death by strangulation then burning. This would have been carried out at the Malt Cross. Tragically, in reality, this was almost certainly part of a property dispute.

Bessie Dunlop

Bessie Dunlop was charged with sorcery, witchcraft, and incantation, trading with charms, and exploiting the people with diabolical magic on November 8, 1576, at the High Court in Ayr. She had been a healer and midwife, relying on old superstitions, traditional folk medicine and charms for helping people. During the trial, Bessie admitted to seeing the spectre of a man named Thomas Reid, who informed her he had died in the Battle of Pinkie. Thomas appears to have served as a kind of'spirit guide' coaching Bessie on how to heal ailments and restore stolen items. Thomas asked her to abandon her religion and enter the land of the fairies, but she declined. Despite her refusal, and despite the fact that Bessie had done no harm to her neighbours, the court judged her guilty and sentenced her to death. Bessie is known as "The Witch O'Dalry", Dalry being a small in Ayrshire. A group of women from the town hope to install a permanent art piece next to the A737 in honour of Bessie. She was only one of 3,837 people known to have faced a formal accusation of witchcraft in Scotland. Around 160 cases involved people from Ayrshire.

Margaret Barclay

Margaret Barclay was strangled and burned at the stake just outside the old burgh boundary,close to where Malcolm Gardens is today in Irvine, in 1618. Her trial resulted in the agonising deaths of herself and three others. They were accused of being involved in a satanic plot that was widely believed to have caused the Irvine vessel The Gift of God to be wrecked off the coast of Cornwall, causing most of the local crew, including Provost Andrew Tran to perish. Margaret was a spirited, high tempered, young woman who firmly denied denied the witchcraft allegations made against her. The most incriminating evidence the Justice Court had against her was that she carried a piece of rowan tree and a colour thread, to make, as she said, her cow to give milk when it failed. To overcome her resolve the inquiry proposed a method of 'gentle torture' as this would take into account her age and sex. This tender torment consisted of placing Margaret's legs in the stocks and loading her bare shins with iron bars until she acknowledged her guilt- the weights were removed only when she promised the inquisition that she would tell them what they wanted to hear. On her last day in court she implored, "All I have confessed was in agony of torture , and before God, all I have spoken is false and untrue". After sentence was passed she was immediately taken to a place of execution. Having made many expressions of religion and repentance, this poor soul met the fate of most convicted witches in Scotland by being strangled at the stake, then having her body reduced to ashes.

Maggie Osborne

Around the early nineteenth century, several stories about Maggie Osborne, a black arts practitioner, circulated in Ayr. They were eventually set down in print, both poetically and in prose. There is no trace of a woman with this name in the surviving records of prosecutions for witchcraft, although there are major gaps in the documents for the period in which she is said to have lived. According to legend Maggie came to harboured a grudge against a certain family, and caused their deaths by making snow envelope their house. The only survivor was a sailor who was at sea at the time, so Maggie was said to have conjured a storm which sank his ship with all hands. Maggie became suspicious that her maid had discovered her secret. Whilst the maid was brewing beer one evening at the Inn Maggie ran, a vicious group of cats entered the outhouse and attacked her, trying to make her fall headlong into the boiling vat. Grabbing a ladle, the girl splashed the steaming liquid over the cats, giving special attention to their leader. The following day, Maggie - reportedly a skilled shape-shfter- stayed in her bed. She was found to have been severlely scalded. Maggie was tried, condemned, and sentenced to be burned at the Malt Cross after the maid told the town's minister, William Adair, everything that she knew. A cross of paving stones exists today in Ayr, in the roadway at the junction of High Street and New Bridge Street, marking the of the Malt Cross.

Isobel Inch

Accused of being in a coven along with Margaret Barclay, Isabel Crawford, and John Stewart in 1618, she was the first of the group to die as a consequence of the torture inflicted upon her by her accusers. She endured several days of horrid interrogation before finally relenting and making a promise to formally confess the following day. However, she attempted suicide by jumping out of her jail cell, which was at the top of the Old Kirk belfry, during the night. Having fallen from a great height, she was severly injured. Despite this, the questioning and pressure to admit guilt continued. Five days later she would succumb to her injuries and die. She never admitted any guilt or being part of a malevolent scheme, and maintained her innocence until her dying breath. The aforementioned John Stewart, an itinerant juggler, also attempted to kill himself prior to his trial whilst incarcerated in the Tolbooth. He convinced the Rev David Dickson, minister of Irvine, who attended him during the trial, that he was so securely tied up that he could not remove his bonnet nor feed himslf. His shackles were loosened and he managed to strangle himself with a piece of hemp used to tie his bonnet.

Isabel Crawford

Another member of the so-called coven in Irvine, which also included Margaret Barclay and Isobel Inch, Isabel Crawford was described as being a woman in the 'full bloom of youth'. Sir Walter Scott wrote about The Irvine Witch Trial 200 years after the fact. Co-accused of harvesting a plot to capsize a ship causing the drowning of most of the crew, Isabel was tried and found guilty. Undergoing the same 'gentle torture' of iron bars piled onto her shins as meted out to Margaret Barclay. Isabel was said to be strong willed though, and managed to suffer through over thirty pounds of iron crushing her legs without uttering a word in pain. She finally gave in to the punishment when her torturers moved the iron bars to different placements on her legs. Only at that point did she agree to confess. She was strangled and burned. Her strong spirit continued until her last moments however. She raged and interuppted the minister, who was reciting prayers whilst overseeing her execution. Also, it was customary for the witch to pardon the executioner- Isabel would do no such thing and pont blank refused.
