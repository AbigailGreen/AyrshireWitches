For Better or Worse

Despite wedding vows, it was fairly normal practice for women accused of witchcraft to face trial under their maiden name, rather then their married surname. It is possible that this was to protect their husbands from the stigma of association with such develish deeds. This may in part explain why it is so difficult to locate formal records of Maggie Osbornes witchcraft trial.

The First Witch

It’s unclear exactly when witches came on the historical scene, but one of the earliest records of a witch is in the Bibl in the book of 1 Samuel, thought be written between 931 B.C. and 721 B.C. It tells the story of when King Saul sought the Witch of Endor to summon the dead prophet Samuel’s spirit to help him defeat the Philistine army. The witch roused Samuel, who then prophesied the death of Saul and his sons. The next day, according to the Bible, Saul’s sons died in battle, and Saul committed suicide.

Wizardry

Although the majority of people accused of witchcraft were female, men were not exempt. Across Europe in the years of witch persecution around 6000 men 10-15% of the total – were executed for witchcraft. In Scotland, records reveal 468 cases of accused men, an example being Alexander Drummond. He had been a healer for 50 years and a campaign to clear his name had a lot of support. Despite this, he was tried, strangled and burned in 1629.

Horsing Around

At Paton Thorn in Irvine the devil in the form of a black foal is said to have appeared to Margaret Barclay and her covenly crew. Horses are associated with many superstitions- The Hungarians and Spanish think black horses are lucky – the French and Bedoin believe the reverse. Black cats are associated with bad luck in many cultures. In Christian faith, Satan is commonly depicted as goat, or creature with cloven hooves.

Modern Witchcraft

Witchcraft did not die out with the last of the executions. Witchcraft, wicca, white witches- they are all present today. Crystals, spells and curses are very much part of current society, venture onto TikTok or any social media platform and you will find many current practicers of the witchy arts. Anyone who believes they have witches in their ancestry or simply is attracted to the otherworldly aspects of the craft can certainly find inspiration and teachings online. From being a feared accusation centuries past, it has evolved into a fullfilling alternativelifestyle for some, not to mention a profitable business venture for others.

Witches in Media

Witches have featured in fairy tales and fiction for centuries. In her earliest incarnations, the witch stories of old hags served warnings. These tales demonised and punished women for attempting to exert power outside the bounds of the domestic sphere. Beyond the fairy tale, women with occult knowledge or simply poor, were the victims of persecution and prosecution in 16th and 17th-century Britain. Nowadays, though, the witch is often praised as a feminist figure , who pushes boundaries, breaks the rules and punishes patriarchal authority. Buffy the Vampire Slayer’s Willow Rosenberg and Disney’s Maleficant are two oft-cited examples of the feminist witch. Other examples of popular witches in modern day theatre include Hannah Abbott from the popular Harry Potter franchise and Agnes and Aja of The Vampire Diaries fame.

Sink or Swim?

In the river near Irvine was a deep pool known as the Witches Plumb where witches were ducked to test if the devil would save them from drowning. This swimming test was conducted all over, the accused were stripped, bound and tossed in the water to to see if they would sink or float. Since witches were believed to have spurned the sacrament of baptism, it was thought that the water would reject their body and prevent them from submerging. According to this logic, an innocent person would sink like a stone, but a witch would simply bob on the surface. The victim typically had a rope tied around their waist so they could be pulled from the water if they sank, but it was common for accidental drowning deaths to occur

The Devils Mark

Witch-hunters often had their suspects stripped and publically examined for signs of an unsightly blemish that witches were said to receive upon making their pact with Satan. This “Devil’s Mark” could supposedly change shape and color, and was believed to be numb and insensitive to pain. Prosecutors might also search for the “witches’ teat,” an extra nipple allegedly used to suckle the witch’s helper animals. In both cases, it was easy for even the most minor physical imperfections to be labeled as the work of the devil himself.

Let Them Eat Cake

A bizarre form of counter-magic, the witch cake was a supernatural dessert used to identify suspected evildoers. In cases of mysterious illness or possession, witch-hunters would take asample of the victim’s urine, mix it with rye-meal and ashes and bake it into a cake. This stomach-turning concoction was then fed to a dog —the “familiars,” or animal helpers of witches, —in the hope that the beast would fall under its spell and reveal the name of the guilty sorcerer
